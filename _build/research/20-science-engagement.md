# Science Stream — Evidence-Based Engagement & Behavior Mechanics for ADHD (with a focus on CHILDREN)

**Prepared for:** Tiny Bubbles (ADHD engagement product)
**Date:** 2026-06-27
**Scope:** Peer-reviewed / PhD-backed mechanisms for engagement and behavior change in ADHD, prioritizing pediatric evidence. Every non-obvious claim is cited with author(s), year, journal, and a URL (DOI/PMID where available). Evidence strength is rated honestly; weak/emerging items are flagged.

> **How to read evidence strength labels**
> - **strong** = converging RCTs and/or meta-analyses, replicated, clinically endorsed (e.g., guideline-grade).
> - **moderate** = multiple controlled studies or a meta-analysis with caveats (heterogeneity, modest effect sizes, or indirect to ADHD/children).
> - **emerging** = early RCTs / proof-of-concept / small samples; promising but not yet settled.
> - **weak** = mostly mechanistic plausibility, surveys, qualitative, or popular sources; controlled evidence thin or null.

## 1. Steeper delay discounting / preference for small-immediate over large-delayed rewards in ADHD

**Claim.** Children and adults with ADHD discount delayed rewards more steeply and choose smaller-but-immediate over larger-but-delayed rewards more often than neurotypical controls. The effect is strongest for **real, experienced delays** (waiting in real time) and **tangible rewards**, and weaker for purely hypothetical "points."

**Key evidence.**
- Marx, I., Hacker, T., Yu, X., Cortese, S., & Sonuga-Barke, E. (2021). *ADHD and the Choice of Small Immediate Over Larger Delayed Rewards: A Comparative Meta-Analysis of Performance on Simple Choice-Delay and Temporal Discounting Paradigms.* **Journal of Attention Disorders, 25(2), 171–187.** https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1087054718772138 — 37 group comparisons (22 simple-choice, 15 temporal-discounting), N ≈ 3,763 (53% ADHD). **Small-to-medium effect sizes** for both paradigms. Crucially, offering **real (vs. hypothetical) rewards almost doubled the odds ratio** of impulsive choice in ADHD — i.e., the deficit is most visible when rewards and waiting are concrete.
- Yu, X., & Sonuga-Barke, E. (2020). *Childhood ADHD and Delayed Reinforcement: A Direct Comparison of Performance on Hypothetical and Real-Time Delay Tasks.* **Journal of Attention Disorders.** https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/1087054716661231 — steeper discounting in children with ADHD on **real-time** tasks, not hypothetical ones.

**Mechanism.** Altered reward valuation / delay aversion: the subjective value of a reward collapses rapidly as delay increases, so distant payoffs fail to motivate behavior now.

**Evidence strength:** strong. **Applies to kids:** yes.

**Design implication (Tiny Bubbles).** Deliver feedback and rewards **immediately at the moment of the target behavior** — sub-second, not end-of-session. Prefer concrete, experienced rewards (animations, sounds, unlocks tied to the action) over abstract "you'll see your score later." If a larger reward must be delayed, **bridge the delay** with visible progress and interim micro-rewards.

## 2. Neurobiology — Dopamine Transfer Deficit (why anticipation and cue-reward pairing matter)

**Claim.** In ADHD, anticipatory dopamine signaling to reward-predicting **cues** is diminished; the dopamine response stays tied to the reward itself rather than transferring to the cues that predict it.

**Key evidence.**
- Tripp, G., & Wickens, J. R. (2008). *Research review: Dopamine transfer deficit: a neurobiological theory of altered reinforcement mechanisms in ADHD.* **Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry, 49(7), 691–704.** DOI 10.1111/j.1469-7610.2007.01851.x — https://acamh.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1469-7610.2007.01851.x ; PMID 18081766 https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18081766/

**Mechanism.** Because the "this cue means reward is coming" signal is weak, behavior is under-motivated by cues and gaps between cue and reward; frequent, salient, immediate reinforcement compensates.

**Evidence strength:** moderate (well-articulated theory backed by animal + human reward-sensitivity data; not a settled causal account). **Applies to kids:** yes (theory developed around childhood ADHD).

**Design implication.** Tightly **pair cues with rewards** and keep the cue→reward gap minimal. Make anticipatory cues vivid (a charging meter, a "reward incoming" animation) so the app supplies the predictive salience the ADHD brain under-generates.

## 3. Token economy / point-and-reward systems for ADHD children

**Claim.** Token/point economies — earning tokens for target behaviors, exchangeable for backup rewards — are an effective, well-established behavioral intervention for children with ADHD, especially when delivered with immediate feedback in structured settings.

**Key evidence.**
- Coles, E. K., Pelham, W. E., Gnagy, E. M., et al. (2005). *A Controlled Evaluation of Behavioral Treatment With Children With ADHD Attending a Summer Treatment Program.* **Journal of Emotional and Behavioral Disorders, 13(2), 99–110.** https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/10634266050130020301 — the Pelham/Fabiano **Summer Treatment Program (STP)** uses an intensive point/token economy with immediate feedback; evaluated across 36 independent studies and judged a **well-established evidence-based treatment**.
- Pelham, W. E., et al. (2000). *The influence of a token economy and methylphenidate on attentive and disruptive behavior during sports with ADHD-diagnosed children.* PMID 11317639 https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11317639/ — token economy reduced disruptive behavior (comparable in magnitude to a moderate methylphenidate dose for some behaviors).
- Fabiano, G. A., Schatz, N. K., Aloe, A. M., Pelham, W. E., et al. (2021). *Comprehensive Meta-Analysis of ADHD Psychosocial Treatments Investigated Within Between-Group Studies.* **Review of Educational Research, 91(5), 718–760.** https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.3102/00346543211025092 — 226 studies; strongest **proximal** effects for behavioral parent training (SMD ≈ **0.70** on parenting behavior) and behavioral school interventions (teacher-rated ADHD/impairment SMD ≈ **0.66–0.72**).

**Mechanism.** Operant contingency management: tokens provide immediate, frequent, tangible secondary reinforcement bridging to delayed backup rewards — directly counteracting delay discounting (§1).

**Evidence strength:** strong. **Applies to kids:** yes (core pediatric evidence base).

**Design implication.** A points/token core loop is squarely evidence-based. Make points **immediate**, **frequent**, and **exchangeable** for child-chosen backup rewards. Pair with a caregiver-managed backup-reward menu so digital points map to real-world reinforcers.

## 4. Reinforcement frequency — continuous first, then "stretch the ratios" to variable schedules

**Claim.** Children with ADHD learn fastest under **dense/continuous** reinforcement; thin/partial schedules degrade their sustained attention and response consistency more than in controls. But behavior learned under **partial/variable** schedules is far more resistant to extinction (the Partial Reinforcement Extinction Effect). The practical resolution is to **start rich and gradually thin** reinforcement.

**Key evidence.**
- Luman, M., Oosterlaan, J., & Sergeant, J. A. (2005). *The impact of reinforcement contingencies on AD/HD: A review and theoretical appraisal.* **Clinical Psychology Review, 25(2), 183–213.** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0272735804001527 — ADHD shows altered responsiveness to reinforcement schedules; performance suffers under partial/infrequent reward and under punishment.
- Hulsbosch, A.-K., et al. (2023). *Instrumental learning and behavioral persistence in children with ADHD: does reinforcement frequency matter?* **Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry.** https://acamh.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/jcpp.13805
- "Stretching the ratios" (weaning from ~100% to sparse reinforcement) preserves persistence while overcoming the partial-reinforcement deficit — see review: *How to Improve Behavioral Parent and Teacher Training for Children with ADHD* (PMC7585566) https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7585566/

**Mechanism.** Dense reinforcement compensates for under-active reward signaling during acquisition; variable schedules later produce extinction-resistant, persistent behavior (the same mechanic slot-machines exploit).

**Evidence strength:** moderate-to-strong. **Applies to kids:** yes.

**Design implication.** New behaviors/skills → **reinforce nearly every success at first.** Once a habit forms, **gradually shift to a variable-ratio schedule** (unpredictable rewards) to sustain engagement and resist drop-off. Do not start with sparse/variable rewards for a brand-new behavior in a child with ADHD.

## 5. Behavioral Parent Training (parent management training) — gold-standard, first-line for young children

**Claim.** Evidence-based behavioral parent training (PTBM) is the **recommended first-line** treatment for preschool-aged ADHD and a core component at all pediatric ages, per AAP guidelines.

**Key evidence.**
- Wolraich, M. L., et al. (2019). *Clinical Practice Guideline for the Diagnosis, Evaluation, and Treatment of ADHD in Children and Adolescents.* **Pediatrics, 144(4), e20192528.** https://publications.aap.org/pediatrics/article/144/4/e20192528/81590 ; PMC7067282 — For children **ages 4 to <6**, clinicians **should prescribe PTBM and/or behavioral classroom interventions as first-line** (**Grade A, strong recommendation**); medication (methylphenidate) only if behavioral treatment is insufficient and impairment is moderate-severe.
- Fabiano et al. (2021), *Review of Educational Research, 91(5), 718–760* (see §3) — quantifies PTBM's strong proximal effects.

**Mechanism.** Parents are trained to restructure antecedents and consequences (praise, attention, token systems, planned ignoring/extinction), strengthening the parent-child relationship and consistently delivering immediate reinforcement.

**Evidence strength:** strong (guideline-grade). **Applies to kids:** yes.

**Design implication.** Build a **caregiver-facing layer**, not just a kid game: coach parents in praise/positive reinforcement, let them set targets and backup rewards, and surface child progress to parents. Position the product as a **delivery vehicle for PTBM principles**, which raises both efficacy and credibility.

## 6. Gamification + Self-Determination Theory (durable vs. brittle motivation)

**Claim.** Gamification produces **small-to-moderate** positive effects on motivation, engagement, and learning. Durable (intrinsic) motivation depends on satisfying the three SDT needs — **autonomy, competence, relatedness**; points/badges/leaderboards mainly boost *extrinsic/controlled* motivation and can undermine intrinsic motivation if experienced as controlling rather than informational.

**Key evidence.**
- Ryan, R. M., & Deci, E. L. (2000). *Self-determination theory and the facilitation of intrinsic motivation, social development, and well-being.* **American Psychologist, 55(1), 68–78.** (Foundational SDT.)
- Sailer, M., & Homner, L. (2020). *The Gamification of Learning: A Meta-analysis.* **Educational Psychology Review, 32(1), 77–112.** https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-019-09498-w — cognitive g ≈ **0.49**, motivational g ≈ **0.36**, behavioral g ≈ **0.25** (small-to-moderate).
- Huang, R., et al. (2023). *Gamification enhances student intrinsic motivation, perceptions of autonomy and relatedness, but minimal impact on competency: a meta-analysis and systematic review.* **Educational Technology Research and Development.** https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11423-023-10337-7
- K-12 motivation meta-analysis (Kurnaz, 2025), **Psychology in the Schools**, pooled g ≈ 0.65 (larger for extrinsic g ≈ 0.71 than intrinsic g ≈ 0.64) https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/pits.70056 — confirms most gamification works as an extrinsic motivator.

**Mechanism.** Game elements scaffold the three needs (choices → autonomy; calibrated challenge + clear feedback → competence; co-play/teams → relatedness). Needs satisfaction predicts higher-quality, more durable motivation.

**Evidence strength:** moderate (robust meta-analytic base in education; most samples are older students — direct evidence in young ADHD children is thinner). **Applies to kids:** partially.

**Design implication.** Don't rely on points alone. Bake in **autonomy** (let the child choose avatars, tasks, reward menus, difficulty), **competence** (adaptive difficulty, clear informational feedback, visible mastery), and **relatedness** (caregiver/peer co-play, shared celebration). Frame rewards as **informational feedback** ("you did it!") rather than controlling ("do this or lose points").

## 7. Time blindness & visual timers — externalizing the broken internal clock

**Claim.** Time-perception deficits — in time **estimation, reproduction, and discrimination** — are a **robust, replicated, core** feature of ADHD across the lifespan. This justifies externalizing time with concrete visual tools (countdown/visual timers). The *specific* commercial visual-timer products (e.g., Time Timer) have a strong theoretical rationale but limited direct RCT validation.

**Key evidence.**
- Marx, I., Cortese, S., Koelch, M. G., & Hacker, T. (2022). *Meta-analysis: Altered Perceptual Timing Abilities in Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder.* **Journal of the American Academy of Child & Adolescent Psychiatry.** https://www.jaacap.org/article/S0890-8567(21)02045-1/abstract — from 2,266 records, 55 studies meta-analyzed; broad, significant timing deficits, especially discriminating brief (sub-second) durations.
- Zheng, Q., Wang, X., Chiu, K. Y., & Shum, K. K. (2022). *Time Perception Deficits in Children and Adolescents with ADHD: A Meta-analysis.* **Journal of Attention Disorders, 26(2), 267–281.** https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/1087054720978557 — confirms the deficit specifically in **children/adolescents**.

**Mechanism.** Impaired internal interval timing + weak temporal foresight; an external visual representation offloads the timing demand from a deficient internal clock (external scaffolding of executive function).

**Evidence strength:** strong for the *deficit*; **weak/emerging** for visual-timer *products* as standalone interventions. **Applies to kids:** yes.

**Design implication.** Provide a **concrete visual representation of elapsed/remaining time** (depleting bar, shrinking color wedge) rather than digits a child must mentally convert. Use it to make abstract durations tangible for both task duration and time-to-reward.

## 8. Implementation intentions ("if-then" plans)

**Claim.** Forming if-then plans ("If situation X arises, then I will do Y") reliably improves goal attainment; the effect holds in children and has been shown to help children with ADHD inhibit distraction and complete tasks.

**Key evidence.**
- Gollwitzer, P. M., & Sheeran, P. (2006). *Implementation Intentions and Goal Achievement: A Meta-Analysis of Effects and Processes.* **Advances in Experimental Social Psychology, 38, 69–119.** — across 94 studies, medium-to-large effect (**d ≈ 0.65**).
- Breitwieser, J., et al. (2025). *The effectiveness of implementation intentions in children: A systematic review and meta-analysis.* **British Journal of Psychology.** DOI 10.1111/bjop.70065 https://bpspsychub.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/bjop.70065 — 52 effect sizes / 42 studies, N = 12,957, mean age 10.7; overall **Hedges g = 0.31** (small-to-medium; high heterogeneity).
- Gawrilow, C., & Gollwitzer, P. M. (2008). *Implementation intentions facilitate response inhibition in children with ADHD.* **Cognitive Therapy and Research, 32, 261–280.** — both distraction-inhibiting and task-facilitating if-then plans helped children with ADHD complete tasks.

**Mechanism.** Pre-committing a specific cue→action link delegates control to the environmental cue, reducing reliance on in-the-moment (impaired) executive initiation.

**Evidence strength:** strong in general adults; moderate in children/ADHD. **Applies to kids:** yes.

**Design implication.** Let kids (with caregiver help) author **"when X happens, I will Y"** plans tied to concrete cues, then have the app **fire the cue** at the right moment. Effects are larger when plans are rehearsed and the user is motivated.

## 9. Task chunking / subgoal segmentation

**Claim.** Breaking tasks into small, visible subgoals lowers cognitive/working-memory load, lowers the barrier to *initiation*, and creates frequent completion points (each a reinforcement opportunity). ADHD's working-memory limits make this especially relevant.

**Key evidence.**
- Working-memory chunking and cognitive-load foundations: Sweller, J. (1988), *Cognitive load during problem solving*, **Cognitive Science, 12(2), 257–285**; adaptive chunking in PFC/basal-ganglia circuits, PMC11870651 https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11870651/
- ADHD-specific working-memory + delay/cognitive-load interaction: *Working memory and decision making in children with ADHD* (PMC7268601) https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7268601/ — memory load amplifies reward-frequency effects more in ADHD children.

**Mechanism.** Decomposition reduces the working-memory span required to hold a multi-step task, and converts one distant payoff into many immediate ones (synergy with §1 and §4).

**Evidence strength:** moderate for the general cognitive principle; **emerging** as a *standalone, manipulated* ADHD intervention (much of the direct ADHD claim is mechanistic/indirect). **Applies to kids:** yes.

**Design implication.** Never present a child with a monolithic task. Auto-decompose goals into micro-steps, show one step at a time, and **reinforce each step's completion** — turning progress itself into a reward.

## 10. Pomodoro / time-boxing with breaks

**Claim.** Structured focus intervals with scheduled breaks can support sustained work, but the evidence is thin and not ADHD-specific; rigid timing can also disrupt flow. Treat as a helpful option, not a proven ADHD intervention.

**Key evidence.**
- Biwer, F., et al. (2025). *Investigating the Effectiveness of Self-Regulated, Pomodoro, and Flowtime Break-Taking Techniques Among Students.* **Behavioral Sciences, 15(7), 861.** https://www.mdpi.com/2076-328X/15/7/861 / PMC12292963.
- Scoping review (2025), **BMC Medical Education**: across 32 studies (only 3 RCTs; total N ≈ 5,270) evidence for Pomodoro on retention is limited and mixed. https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12909-025-08001-0
- Micro-break meta-analysis: short (<10 min) breaks aid well-being; longer breaks aid recovery; passive screen breaks can hurt refocus.

**Mechanism.** Time-boxing externalizes time (links to §7), bounds effort to a tolerable window, and schedules recovery before fatigue accrues.

**Evidence strength:** weak/emerging (especially for children with ADHD). **Applies to kids:** partially.

**Design implication.** Offer **short, flexible focus intervals + restorative breaks** as an *optional* mode. Keep intervals adjustable (don't force 25 min on a young child), and use restorative (movement) rather than passive screen breaks. Don't over-claim.

## 11. Body doubling / co-regulation

**Claim.** Working alongside another present person ("body doubling") is widely reported by ADHD users to aid task initiation, focus, and completion. Mechanisms (social facilitation, accountability, co-regulation) are plausible, but **controlled experimental evidence is thin and partly null** — current support is largely survey/qualitative.

**Key evidence.**
- Qualitative/HCI work on body doubling for neurodivergent users (ACM accessibility venues, 2023–2024) reports strong perceived benefit for initiation and persistence; VR body-doubling design study: https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.12153
- Focusmate user-survey data report large *self-reported* productivity gains (≈143% overall; ≈161% neurodivergent) — note: **uncontrolled self-report.**
- Controlled lab studies to date have **not** shown a significant effect of a body-double on performance even when participants found it motivating (mechanism unconfirmed).

**Mechanism.** Social facilitation, implicit accountability, nervous-system co-regulation/entrainment, and a commitment device that turns "sometime" into "now."

**Evidence strength:** weak (popular + qualitative strong; controlled evidence mixed/null). **Applies to kids:** limited (most evidence is adults/teens).

**Design implication.** Offer **co-play / shared sessions** (with a caregiver or peer) and scheduled "do-it-together" moments. Frame as an engagement feature, not a clinically proven mechanism. For children, a present caregiver doubles as the body double *and* the reinforcement agent (§5).

## 12. Digital therapeutic with RCT evidence — EndeavorRx / AKL-T01 (Project: EVO lineage)

**Claim.** EndeavorRx is an FDA-authorized, video-game-style digital therapeutic. Its pivotal double-blind RCT (STARS-ADHD) **met its primary endpoint** — improved objective attention (TOVA Attention Performance Index) — but **did NOT** show significant between-group improvement on parent- or clinician-rated ADHD symptoms/impairment. Engagement-driven adaptive gameplay can improve a lab attention measure; translation to real-world symptom change is unproven.

**Key evidence.**
- Kollins, S. H., DeLoss, D. J., Cañadas, E., Lutz, J., Findling, R. L., Keefe, R. S. E., Epstein, J. N., Cutler, A. J., & Faraone, S. V. (2020). *A novel digital intervention for actively reducing severity of paediatric ADHD (STARS-ADHD): a randomised controlled trial.* **The Lancet Digital Health, 2(4), e168–e178.** DOI 10.1016/S2589-7500(20)30017-0 ; PMID 33334505 https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33334505/
  - Design: randomized, double-blind, parallel-group; **N = 348** (180 intervention / 168 control), **ages 8–12**, unmedicated; 25 min/day, 5 days/week, 4 weeks.
  - Primary: TOVA API mean change 0.93 (intervention) vs 0.03 (control); **between-group difference ~0.88–0.90, 95% CI 0.24–1.49, p = 0.0060.**
  - **Limitation (be honest):** **no significant between-group differences** on secondary measures — ADHD-RS (inattention/hyperactivity), Impairment Rating Scale, BRIEF, or CGI-I. Objective attention improved; clinician/parent-rated symptoms did not differ from control.

**Mechanism.** Adaptive, closed-loop sensorimotor/attention game targeting attentional control with engaging audiovisual feedback and difficulty scaling.

**Evidence strength:** moderate (positive, rigorous primary endpoint) — but with an important **efficacy-translation caveat** (no symptom-rating advantage). **Applies to kids:** yes (8–12; label later extended to 13–17).

**Design implication.** Validates that **adaptive difficulty + tight reward feedback loops** can engage children with ADHD and move an attention metric. But it is a cautionary tale: **don't over-promise symptom/impairment change** from a game alone; pair engagement mechanics with behavioral scaffolding (§3, §5) for real-world impact.

## 13. Biofeedback emotion-regulation games — Mightier / RAGE-Control / "Regulate"

**Claim.** Heart-rate biofeedback games that reward physiological down-regulation during challenging play can improve anger control, aggression, and emotion regulation in children/teens (some with ADHD). Promising RCT signal, but small samples.

**Key evidence.**
- Ducharme, P., Kahn, J., Vaudreuil, C., et al. (2021). *A "Proof of Concept" Randomized Controlled Trial of a Video Game Requiring Emotional Regulation to Augment Anger Control Training (RAGE-Control).* **Frontiers in Psychiatry, 12, 591906.** DOI 10.3389/fpsyt.2021.591906 ; PMID 34539455 https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8440816/
  - Double-blind RCT, **N = 40** (ages 10–17; sample included ODD, ADHD, depression/anxiety). Active biofeedback (spaceship fires blanks if HR exceeds baseline +7 bpm) vs sham, both added to Anger Control Training.
  - Effect sizes vs sham: **aggression (MOAS) ES = 0.55, p = 0.015; oppositionality (DBDRS) ES = 0.48, p = 0.032; global severity (CGI-S) ES = 0.51, p = 0.023.** (Self-reported anger: no between-group difference.)
- Systematic review of biofeedback digital games (including Mightier), 16 studies: reduced internalizing/externalizing symptoms and promoted emotion regulation, especially in clinical populations. PMC12718225 https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12718225/

**Mechanism.** Real-time interoceptive feedback trains down-regulation of physiological arousal under provocation; "stay calm to win" makes regulation the win condition.

**Evidence strength:** emerging-to-moderate (RCT signal with medium effect sizes, but small Ns; broader Mightier home-use evidence is partly uncontrolled/parent-report). **Applies to kids:** yes.

**Design implication.** If targeting **emotional regulation/frustration tolerance** (common in pediatric ADHD), a "**calm-to-progress**" biofeedback or breathing-gated mechanic has real evidence behind it. Even without hardware HR, a self-paced "regulate then proceed" loop is defensible.

## 14. RISK — punishment, loss, and streak-loss can demotivate ADHD users

**Claim.** Children with ADHD respond **less effectively to punishment** and show altered, blunted processing of negative feedback; performance and persistence suffer under punishment/loss-based contingencies relative to reward-based ones. Punitive game mechanics — streak loss, point deduction, public failure — risk demotivation, shame, and disengagement.

**Key evidence.**
- Luman, M., Oosterlaan, J., & Sergeant, J. A. (2005). *The impact of reinforcement contingencies on AD/HD.* **Clinical Psychology Review, 25(2), 183–213.** (see §4) — punishment is a less reliable behavior-change lever in ADHD; reward generally outperforms.
- Reward/punishment sensitivity moderation study (2018), **Research on Child and Adolescent Psychopathology / J. Abnormal Child Psychology**, DOI 10.1007/s10802-018-0421-y https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10802-018-0421-y — reward/punishment sensitivity interacts with parenting to shape ADHD symptom expression; **not all children benefit from contingency-based (especially punitive) treatments.**
- Feedback-related ERP work: children with ADHD show altered/under-modulated feedback-related negativity to outcome valence (e.g., PLOS One, PMC4059686) https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4059686/ — deficient detection of the motivational significance of negative feedback.

**Mechanism.** Blunted/altered processing of negative outcomes + heightened delay aversion means losses neither teach efficiently nor motivate; loss of accumulated progress (streaks) can trigger disproportionate frustration and avoidance.

**Evidence strength:** moderate. **Applies to kids:** yes.

**Design implication.** **Default to positive reinforcement; minimize punishment and loss.** Avoid hard streak-loss and point-clawbacks. Use **streak freezes / forgiveness mechanics**, frame setbacks neutrally ("let's try again" not "you lost"), and make the salient signal the *gain*, never the penalty. Keep failure private and low-stakes.

## 15. Novelty / stimulation-seeking and variable, surprising rewards

**Claim.** ADHD is associated with elevated stimulation-/sensation-seeking and under-arousal; **novel and unpredictable** stimuli engage attention disproportionately. Varying and surprising rewards sustain engagement better than static, predictable ones.

**Key evidence.**
- Antrop, I., et al. (2006). *Stimulation seeking and hyperactivity in children with ADHD.* **Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry, 47(11), 1152–1158.** — children with ADHD seek stimulation, consistent with an under-arousal / optimal-stimulation account.
- Sergeant, J. A. (2005). *Modeling attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder: a critical appraisal of the cognitive-energetic model.* **Biological Psychiatry, 57(11), 1248–1255.** — state-regulation/energetic account: arousal/activation deficits explain need for novelty and faster event rates.
- Tripp & Wickens (2008) (§2) — altered dopaminergic reward signaling underpins novelty/reward responsivity.

**Mechanism.** Novelty and prediction error elicit phasic dopamine responses that transiently normalize under-stimulated attentional systems; variability (variable-ratio) maintains the prediction-error signal (synergy with §4).

**Evidence strength:** moderate/emerging. **Applies to kids:** yes.

**Design implication.** **Rotate and vary** rewards, content, and visuals; introduce surprise/mystery rewards; avoid sameness that breeds boredom. Pair novelty with the variable-ratio schedule (§4) — unpredictable, varied rewards are the most engagement-robust configuration for ADHD.

## Cross-cutting design synthesis for Tiny Bubbles

1. **Immediacy is non-negotiable.** Reinforce *at the moment* of behavior (delay discounting + dopamine transfer deficit). [§1, §2]
2. **Points/tokens core loop is evidence-based** — but tie digital points to caregiver-managed real-world backup rewards. [§3, §5]
3. **Schedule reinforcement deliberately:** continuous during learning → gradually thin to variable-ratio for durability. [§4]
4. **Ship a caregiver layer** that operationalizes behavioral parent training; this is the strongest pediatric evidence base. [§5]
5. **Build for SDT, not just points:** autonomy (choice), competence (adaptive difficulty + informational feedback), relatedness (co-play). [§6]
6. **Externalize time** with concrete visual timers. [§7]
7. **Scaffold execution:** if-then plans + auto task-chunking with per-step reinforcement. [§8, §9]
8. **Avoid punitive mechanics:** no harsh streak loss; use forgiveness/freezes; keep failure private. [§14]
9. **Vary and surprise** rewards to exploit novelty-seeking. [§15]
10. **Don't over-promise.** Engagement/attention gains (EndeavorRx) ≠ symptom/impairment change; emotion-regulation biofeedback is promising but small-sample. Be honest in marketing/clinical claims. [§12, §13]

## Honesty ledger — what is NOT well-proven

- **Visual-timer products** specifically (strong rationale, weak product-level RCT). [§7]
- **Pomodoro/time-boxing** for ADHD children (thin, mixed, mostly non-ADHD samples). [§10]
- **Body doubling** (popular and qualitatively strong; controlled effects mixed/null; little pediatric data). [§11]
- **Task chunking as a manipulated standalone ADHD intervention** (principle solid; direct ADHD trials sparse). [§9]
- **EndeavorRx symptom impact** (objective attention improved; parent/clinician symptom ratings did not differ from control). [§12]
- **Mightier/biofeedback** (RCT signal real but small Ns; some home-use evidence uncontrolled). [§13]
