# Market Stream: ADHD / Executive-Function Apps for KIDS

**Prepared:** 2026-06-27
**Context:** Competitive/market research for "Tiny Bubbles," an ADHD app for kids. This stream focuses specifically on apps where the *child* is the user (or co-user with a parent), not adult-only ADHD tools.

> **TL;DR for product strategy**
> - The proven, dominant model for kids' ADHD apps is the **"two-app contingency-management loop"**: parent assigns real-world tasks in a parent app → child completes offline → parent verifies → child earns in-game tokens to raise/customize a virtual pet or avatar. **Joon** is the category leader and the closest analog to Tiny Bubbles.
> - **Subscription (SaaS) is the winning monetization model** ($80–$130/yr typical). The **prescription digital-therapeutic model (EndeavorRx/Akili) collapsed commercially** — a critical cautionary tale: clinically validated, FDA-cleared, but payers wouldn't reimburse and the company was sold for parts.
> - The hardest, most-cited product problem across every app is **novelty decay / engagement drop-off at 4–8 weeks**. Sustained engagement (not acquisition) is the real battleground.
> - **Hardware bundles (Mightier's heart sensor, Goally's tablet) raise price and clinical credibility but cap scale**; pure-software apps scale but struggle to differentiate and retain.

---

## 1. Market segmentation

Kids' ADHD/executive-function apps cluster into five functional archetypes. Tiny Bubbles sits squarely in archetype A.

| Archetype | What it does | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **A. Parent-assigned task + virtual-pet/avatar reward loop** (contingency management gamified) | Parent sets chores/routines; kid earns tokens to grow a pet/character | **Joon**, ChoreMonster (defunct), OurHome, EpicWin, Habitica (family) |
| **B. Visual routines / time-management scaffolding** | Step-by-step visual schedules, timers, transitions | **Brili Routines**, **Goally**, Cozi (family org) |
| **C. Biofeedback emotional-regulation games** | Kid plays a game gated by their own heart rate | **Mightier** |
| **D. Cognitive-training "digital therapeutic"** | Adaptive attention-training video game; clinical/Rx | **EndeavorRx / EndeavorOTC (Akili)** |
| **E. Special-education / skill-building game libraries** | Curricula of learning mini-games | **Otsimo** |

## 2. Joon — the category leader (closest analog to Tiny Bubbles)

**Joon, Inc.** "Joon: Kids ADHD Chore Tracker" / "Joon Pet Game." Founded 2020, San Francisco, by Brad Brenner, Isaac Eaves, and Kevin Bunarjo. ([dot.LA / Crunchbase via search](https://www.crunchbase.com/organization/joon-motivate-your-kids); [Tracxn](https://tracxn.com/d/companies/joon/__ufDwnuDR-RxF99hHytGNhTqLBJUP7S2dyodGccse5wA))

> Note: There are several unrelated companies named "Joon." The one relevant here is the kids' ADHD app at **joonapp.io** (a.k.a. Joon Pet Game). It is *distinct* from "Joon Care"/Joon Health (joonhealth.co), a separate teen teletherapy provider. Effectiveness stats like "78% anxiety returned to normal" belong to the teletherapy company, **not** the kids' app — do not conflate them.

- **Target age:** 6–12 (most effective range); marketed for ADHD, ASD, ODD, anxiety, depression. ([joonapp.io](https://www.joonapp.io/); [choosingtherapy](https://www.choosingtherapy.com/joon-app-review/))
- **What the kid actually does:** The child never sees a "chore list." They open the **Joon Pet Game** and care for a virtual pet called a **Doter** — feeding it, washing it, buying accessories with in-game coins, and unlocking new "lands." Chores are reframed as **quests** inside a game world. ([screensdesign](https://screensdesign.com/showcase/joon-kids-adhd-chore-tracker); [timily review](https://timily.app/guides/joon-app-review/))
- **Reward/engagement loop (the core mechanic):** Two linked apps. **Parent app:** parent creates tasks/quests (templated by age or custom), schedules them, and **approves completion**. **Kid app:** completing a parent-approved quest pays out **coins** → coins feed/grow/customize the Doter → an incomplete-task state degrades the pet's mood/health (loss-aversion nudge). Parents can also let kids redeem coins for **real-world rewards** (screen time, outings). This is textbook **contingency management / token economy** with a variable-reward game wrapper. ([timily](https://timily.app/guides/joon-app-review/))
- **Pricing & business model:** Freemium SaaS subscription. **$12.99/month or ~$79.99–$89.99/year**, with a **7-day free trial**; reduced rates for families in need on request. App Store lists in-app purchases ranging **$12.99–$149.99** (multi-year/family tiers). No real-money in-app purchases for kids beyond the subscription. ([choosingtherapy](https://www.choosingtherapy.com/joon-app-review/); [Apple App Store](https://apps.apple.com/us/app/joon-kids-adhd-chore-tracker/id1482225056))
- **Downloads / traction:** Launched 2022; **500,000+ users** and **~18,000 paying subscribers**; markets "200,000+ families," "1M+ tasks completed," and "90% of kids complete all assigned tasks." Raised **$6.4M** in Oct 2023 (led by **MaC Venture Capital** and **Dune Ventures**); ~$6.4–6.8M total raised. App Store rating **4.7 / 5 across ~6,600 ratings**. ([Tracxn](https://tracxn.com/d/companies/joon/__ufDwnuDR-RxF99hHytGNhTqLBJUP7S2dyodGccse5wA); [Apple App Store](https://apps.apple.com/us/app/joon-kids-adhd-chore-tracker/id1482225056); [joonapp.io](https://www.joonapp.io/))
- **Clinical/scientific backing:** Marketed as "backed by child psychologists, occupational therapists, and teachers," but as of mid-2026 there are **no peer-reviewed efficacy publications** for the kids' app. There **is** a registered RCT: **NCT06447909**, "Randomized Controlled Trial of a Behavioral Training App," **sponsored by New York University with Joon, Inc. as collaborator**. Design: randomized, single-blind (participant), parallel, **n≈100**, **ages 6–12**; the treatment app = Joon's virtual-pet contingency-reward system; the placebo = an identical app **with the contingency reward system removed**. Primary outcomes: IOWA Conners Rating Scale, Disruptive Behavior Disorders (DBD) Rating Scale (Pelham et al., 1992), Impairment Rating Scale (Pelham, Fabiano & Massetti, 2005). Started Mar 2024, estimated primary completion Aug 2024; **status "unknown/recruiting," no results posted yet**. ([ClinicalTrials.gov NCT06447909](https://clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT06447909))
- **Parent sentiment — what works:** "Our son woke up BEFORE HIS ALARM to get started on his Joon quests"; "the immediate dopamine hit she needs to push through brushing teeth, getting dressed"; "I'm not having to remind/nag nearly as often." The **immediate, in-app reward + game framing** is the repeatedly praised mechanic. ([Apple App Store reviews](https://apps.apple.com/us/app/joon-kids-adhd-chore-tracker/id1482225056))
- **Parent sentiment — what frustrates:** The single most common complaint is **engagement decay after 4–8 weeks** (novelty wears off), which makes the subscription feel expensive if the kid abandons it. Other gripes: **price is high relative to alternatives**, **no screen-time controls**, **requires consistent parent task-verification** (if the parent disengages, the loop breaks), and the **narrow effective age band** (loses teens). ([timily](https://timily.app/guides/joon-app-review/); [choosingtherapy](https://www.choosingtherapy.com/joon-app-review/))

**Why this matters for Tiny Bubbles:** Joon has effectively proven product-market fit for the exact loop Tiny Bubbles is pursuing, *and* its weaknesses (novelty decay, parent-dependency, no screen-time guardrails, age cliff) are the open white space to attack.

## 3. Brili Routines (Archetype B — visual routines)

- **Target age / users:** Built for the ADHD/ADD community; used for kids and adults. Strong use case is kids' morning/bedtime routines (a separate "Brili Routines – Visual Timer" kids product and an adult product exist). ([brili.com](https://brili.com/how-it-works/))
- **What the kid does:** Follows a **visual, timed sequence** of routine steps; the app shows the current step in the context of the whole routine, with a countdown timer, gentle audible prompts, and alarms; the child "swipes" each step as done. ([brili.com](https://brili.com/how-it-works/); [Common Sense Media](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/app-reviews/brili-routines))
- **Reward/engagement loop:** Time-boxed task completion with light gamification (points/rewards) and a "beat the clock" feel. Claims users save ~30 min per routine. ([brili.com](https://brili.com/))
- **Pricing & model:** Freemium subscription, **~$19.99/year** for full features (some tiers/sources cite up to ~$50/year). ([Educational App Store](https://www.educationalappstore.com/app/brili-routines-visual-timer))
- **Traction:** Android "Visual Timer" rated **~4.0 / 5 across ~200 ratings**; modest scale vs Joon. ([justuseapp reviews](https://justuseapp.com/en/app/952895165/brili-routines-visual-timer/reviews))
- **Clinical backing:** Self-described as "based on the latest ADHD research" and CBT best practices; **no published RCT.** ([brili.com](https://brili.com/))
- **Parent sentiment:** Praised for transforming chaotic mornings ("gets ready in half the time, no reminders"). Key frustration: kids can **swipe-complete without actually doing the task** (no verification), plus technical bugs. ([Common Sense Media](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/app-reviews/brili-routines))

## 4. Goally (Archetype B + hardware)

- **Target age / users:** Marketed for **ages ~2–8** (kids with ADHD, autism, developmental/executive-function needs) building independence and emotional regulation. ([getgoally.com](https://getgoally.com/))
- **What the kid does:** Uses a **distraction-free, purpose-built kids tablet** (no YouTube, no ads, no browser) running Goally's app suite: visual routines/chores (**CoPilot**), emotional regulation (**Mood Tuner**), life-skills/video modeling (**Goal Mine**), early academics, and money basics (**Polar Bank**). Step-by-step interactive routines with token rewards and timers. ([getgoally.com](https://getgoally.com/); [Educational App Store](https://www.educationalappstore.com/app/goally))
- **Reward/engagement loop:** Token-based rewards, video modeling, built-in timers; parent dashboard to customize routines and track progress; 100+ pre-made routine templates. ([Educational App Store](https://www.educationalappstore.com/app/goally))
- **Pricing & model:** **Hardware + subscription.** Tablet bundle **~$369–$449 for the first year** (includes Goally apps + Goally TV for 12 months, free US shipping, 90-day guarantee, one coaching call); ongoing subscription thereafter (historically ~$15–$20/mo). An app-only path has existed at points. ([getgoally.com](https://getgoally.com/); [getgoally.com/pricing](https://getgoally.com/pricing/))
- **Traction / status:** Still operating and selling tablets as of 2026 (no confirmed shutdown). Niche, higher-price positioning vs pure apps. ([getgoally.com](https://getgoally.com/))
- **Clinical backing:** Built on established visual-schedule / token-economy and video-modeling practices for autism/ADHD; **no published RCT for the product itself.**
- **Takeaway:** The **closed-device** model (no ads/algorithmic feeds) is a strong parent-trust differentiator but raises price and caps addressable market vs a phone app.

## 5. Mightier (Archetype C — biofeedback emotional regulation)

Made by **Neuromotion Labs**, spun out of **Boston Children's Hospital** (2016); the underlying tech is "RAGE-Control" (Regulate And Gain Emotional Control).

- **Target age / conditions:** **Ages 6–14**; positioned for anxiety, ADHD, anger, autism, outbursts, ODD. ([mightier.com](https://www.mightier.com/))
- **What the kid does:** Wears a **rechargeable heart-rate sensor** and plays mobile games. The child's **live heart rate is shown on-screen** (the "Gizmo": blue = calm, grey, red = aroused). As the child gets dysregulated (heart rate climbs into the red), the **game gets harder/penalizes**, so winning requires practicing real calming skills (deep breathing, etc.) under stress. ([mightier.com](https://www.mightier.com/); [autismdad review](https://theautismdad.com/2018/08/28/kids-learn-self-regulation-through-gaming-with-mightier-review/))
- **Reward/engagement loop:** Mastery loop — calmer body = better game performance; 25+ games, monthly mailed "skill packs," conversation cards, parent app. Recommended ~**45 min/week (3 sessions) for ≥90 days**. ([mightier.com/plans](https://www.mightier.com/plans))
- **Pricing & model:** Subscription with included hardware: **$40/mo**, **$204/6-mo ($34/mo)**, or **$336/yr ($28/mo)**; all tiers include the heart sensor, app, skill packs, conversation cards, and parent app. Optional **GamePad +$50**. HSA/FSA accepted; 90-day money-back guarantee; one membership covers multiple kids. ([mightier.com/plans](https://www.mightier.com/plans))
- **Clinical backing (the strongest in this set besides Akili):**
  - **RCT (proof-of-concept):** Ducharme P, Kahn J, Vaudreuil C, Gonzalez-Heydrich J, et al. (2021). *"A 'Proof of Concept' Randomized Controlled Trial of a Video Game Requiring Emotional Regulation to Augment Anger Control Training."* **Frontiers in Psychiatry.** PMID **34539455**; PMC8440816. Found increased heart-rate control during gameplay associated with **decreased aggression and oppositional behavior**. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34539455/))
  - **Large descriptive study (not an RCT):** Wintner J, Waters AC, Peechatka A, Gonzalez-Heydrich J, Kahn J (2022). *Evaluation of a scalable online videogame-based biofeedback program to improve emotion regulation: a descriptive study assessing parent perspectives.* **Internet Interventions.** PMC8960945. **n=1,045 players from 1,426 families**; open-label, retrospective, **no control group**; parents reported increased use of emotion-regulation skills (79% wks 1–8 → 87% wks 9–16). Authors explicitly flag selection bias and absence of randomization/standardized follow-up. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8960945/))
  - Marketing claims: "87% see positive change in 90 days"; for ADHD specifically, after 3 months "84% of parents report improvements in anger/frustration/aggression" and "71% report improvements in impulsivity/hyperactivity." ([mightier.com](https://www.mightier.com/resources/mightier-for-children-with-adhd/))
- **Parent sentiment:** Praised for **reducing meltdowns** and giving kids transferable calming skills; frustrations center on **price, the hassle/charging of the sensor**, and the fact that benefits require consistent multi-month use.

## 6. EndeavorRx / EndeavorOTC — Akili (Archetype D) — the CAUTIONARY TALE

The most clinically credentialed product in the category and the clearest business-model failure. **Required reading for any kids' ADHD startup considering the "medical/Rx" route.**

- **What it is / who it's for:** The **first FDA-cleared video-game treatment**, cleared via the **de novo pathway in June 2020** for inattentive/combined-type ADHD in children **ages 8–12**; **expanded to ages 8–17 in December 2023**. ([AAP News](https://publications.aap.org/aapnews/news/9096/FDA-approves-digital-game-for-children-with-ADHD); [Managed Healthcare Executive](https://www.managedhealthcareexecutive.com/view/fda-expands-akili-s-endeavorrx-game-based-digital-therapy-for-adhd-eligibility-to-ages-8-17))
- **What the kid does:** A **racing/obstacle action game (AKL-T01)** with an adaptive algorithm that targets attentional control by forcing rapid multitasking (navigate + respond to targets). Dose: **~25 min/day, 5 days/week, ≥4 weeks.** ([endeavorrx.com/faq](https://www.endeavorrx.com/faq/))
- **Clinical backing (the gold standard here):** **STARS-ADHD** — Kollins SH, DeLoss DJ, Cañadas E, Lutz J, Findling RL, Keefe RSE, Epstein JN, Cutler AJ, Faraone SV (2020). *"A novel digital intervention for actively reducing severity of paediatric ADHD (STARS-ADHD): a randomised controlled trial."* **The Lancet Digital Health 2(4):e168–e178.** PMID **33334505**; DOI **10.1016/S2589-7500(20)30017-0**. Double-blind, **n=348**, ages 8–12, EndeavorRx vs a word-game digital control. Primary outcome (change in **TOVA Attention Performance Index**): **0.93 vs 0.03, p=0.006**; ~1/3 of treated kids no longer had a measurable attention deficit on ≥1 objective measure. The 8–17 expansion rested on a further RCT of **162 adolescents**. ([Lancet Digital Health](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/landig/article/PIIS2589-7500(20)30017-0/fulltext); [adolescent RCT, PMC11127175](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11127175/)). Caveat widely noted by clinicians: improvements were on **computerized attention metrics (TOVA)**, with **more modest effects on day-to-day clinician/parent-rated symptoms**.
- **Business-model collapse (the lesson):**
  - As a **prescription digital therapeutic (PDT)**, Akili could not get payers/insurers to reimburse. Q2 2023: **just ~$114,000 in revenue against ~$15.3M in expenses.** ([STAT](https://www.statnews.com/2023/09/13/akili-endeavorrx-prescription-business-model-lay-offs/))
  - **Sept 2023:** Akili **abandoned the prescription model** and **laid off ~40%** of staff, pivoting toward a direct-to-consumer/OTC approach. ([Healthcare Brew](https://www.healthcare-brew.com/stories/2023/09/15/digital-therapeutics-company-akili-ditches-prescription-business-model); [STAT](https://www.statnews.com/2023/09/13/akili-endeavorrx-prescription-business-model-lay-offs/))
  - **2024:** Akili (a SPAC-listed company) was **taken private by Virtual Therapeutics for ~$34M (~$0.434/share)**; tender offer completed **July 2, 2024.** This is the same payer-reimbursement trap that **bankrupted Pear Therapeutics** the prior year. ([BioPharma Dive](https://www.biopharmadive.com/news/akili-sell-34m-virtual-therapeutics-digital/717576/); [InsideArbitrage](https://www.insidearbitrage.com/2024/05/virtual-therapeutics-set-to-acquire-akili-in-an-all-cash-deal/))
- **Current pricing/availability (mid-2026):**
  - **EndeavorRx (prescription, kids 8–17):** **$99 / 30-day prescription**, FSA/HSA-eligible, requires a clinician prescription; a free demo exists. ([endeavorrx.com/faq](https://www.endeavorrx.com/faq/); [SingleCare](https://www.singlecare.com/prescription/endeavorrx))
  - **EndeavorOTC (no prescription, adults 18+):** free to download, freemium with in-app purchases **~$69.99/3mo, $99.99/6mo, $129.99/yr.** ([pharmaphorum](https://pharmaphorum.com/news/akili-launches-otc-version-digital-therapeutic-adhd); [choosingtherapy](https://www.choosingtherapy.com/endeavor-otc-review/))
- **Lesson for Tiny Bubbles:** FDA clearance and a Lancet RCT did **not** create a viable business because reimbursement never materialized. A **direct-to-consumer subscription** (Joon/Mightier model) has proven far more commercially durable for kids' ADHD than the Rx/insurance route. If clinical validation is pursued, pair it with a **consumer-pay** model, not payer dependence.

## 7. Otsimo (Archetype E — special-ed game library)

- **Target users:** Kids with autism, ADHD, Down syndrome, and learning/attention differences; special education and AAC/speech-therapy companion apps. ([otsimo.com](https://otsimo.com/en/))
- **What the kid does:** Plays through **100+ educational mini-games** on a personalized curriculum (no ads, offline-capable), plus AAC communication and social storybooks. ([Educational App Store](https://www.educationalappstore.com/app/otsimo-special-education-aba))
- **Pricing & model:** Freemium subscription — **~$20.99/month, ~$13.75/mo billed annually, or $229.99 lifetime**; 7-day free trial. ([EdTech Impact](https://edtechimpact.com/products/otsimo-special-education/))
- **Clinical backing:** Curriculum-based, aligned to special-ed practice; no major RCT surfaced.
- **Relevance:** Less directly comparable to Tiny Bubbles (it's a learning-content library, not a routine/behavior loop), but notable for the **lifetime-purchase option** and special-needs positioning.

## 8. Chore/family apps used for ADHD kids (Archetype A, adjacent)

These are mainstream family apps, not ADHD-specific, but parents repurpose them — useful for understanding monetization and why a *dedicated* ADHD app wins.

- **ChoreMonster:** Free kids' chore app (ages 4–12) with a points-for-real-rewards system and 100+ collectible monsters. **Now defunct (~2018)**; the team (FamilyTech) folded it, and **FamZoo** absorbed the niche by combining chore tracking with family banking. Cautionary note on free-only chore apps lacking a durable revenue model. ([Kidscreen](https://kidscreen.com/2017/02/16/how-familytechs-apps-are-sweeping-the-chores-trend/); [mybubbaandme](http://mybubbaandme.com/contributing/what-is-our-home-app/))
- **OurHome:** Free all-in-one household manager (chores + points + grocery/meal/calendar). **Completely free with no monetization** in place — a frequently cited example of an unmonetized, hard-to-sustain model. ([mybubbaandme](http://mybubbaandme.com/contributing/what-is-our-home-app/))
- **Cozi:** Family organizer (shared calendar, lists, light to-dos). Free with **Cozi Gold ad-free premium at $29.99/yr.** Not behavior-change focused. ([kidkarma comparison](https://kidkarma.app/compare/best-chore-apps/))

## 9. EpicWin (Archetype A pioneer — gamified to-do)

- **What it is:** An early (2010–11) **RPG-style to-do app**: completing real tasks earns **XP** to level up a fantasy avatar across stats (Strength, Stamina, Intellect, Social, Spirit); the avatar visually "destroys" each completed task. ([Google Play](https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?hl=en_IE&id=com.supermono.epicwin); [Common Sense Media](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/app-reviews/epicwin))
- **Audience/age:** General productivity (teens/adults), not ADHD-specific or young-kid-specific; **no parent-verification layer.**
- **Traction/status:** Cited at **~400k users**; a paid-app pioneer that has been **largely unmaintained** for years. ([gamifylist](https://gamifylist.com/app/epic-win))
- **Relevance:** Proof that the **task→XP→avatar-progression loop is engaging**, but also that **novelty alone doesn't sustain** a product without ongoing content, social, or parent-loop reinforcement.

## 10. Habitica (Archetype A — family/RPG, DIY)

- **What it is:** Free, open-ish **retro-RPG habit tracker**: create an avatar, log Habits/Dailies/To-Dos; completing them yields gold, XP, and gear; missing Dailies damages your avatar (loss aversion). Active **ADHD support guilds**. ([Google Play](https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.habitrpg.android.habitica); [choosingtherapy](https://www.choosingtherapy.com/habitica-app-review/))
- **Kids/family use:** Not built for young kids, but families adopt it together (parents + kids set each other's habits) — a notable **DIY ADHD pattern.** Parent guides exist on running it as a family system. ([Habitica Wiki: Adapting for ADHD](https://habitica.fandom.com/wiki/Adapting_Habitica_for_ADHD); [Medium: hacking kids' habits](https://medium.com/@redface/hacking-your-kids-habits-via-gamification-our-recipe-explained-habitica-com-1022ce7a97bd))
- **Pricing & model:** Freemium subscription **~$4.99–$47.99** depending on tier (plus cosmetic gem purchases). ([choosingtherapy](https://www.choosingtherapy.com/habitica-app-review/))
- **Relevance:** Validates that the **avatar/RPG reward loop drives ADHD engagement**, and that **social/group accountability** (guilds, party quests) is a retention lever Joon-style apps mostly lack. Weakness: too text/menu-heavy and self-directed for young kids.

## 11. "Thrive" — note on ambiguity

There is **no prominent, dedicated kids' ADHD app clearly branded "Thrive"** in the current market; the name maps to several unrelated products (e.g., Thrive Global wellbeing; various habit apps). In "best ADHD apps" roundups, the recurring kids' picks are **Joon, EndeavorRx, Brili, Goally, Mightier**, not a "Thrive." ([choosingtherapy roundup](https://www.choosingtherapy.com/best-adhd-apps/); [familyeducation](https://www.familyeducation.com/kids/neurodiversity/adhd/22-super-helpful-apps-for-kids/)). **Recommendation:** if a specific "Thrive" product is intended, confirm the exact app/developer before treating it as a competitor — current evidence suggests it is not a category player for kids.

## 12. PROVEN engagement loops for KIDS' ADHD apps

Synthesized across the products above plus the gamification/ADHD literature:

1. **Parent-assigned task → offline completion → parent verification → in-app token (the contingency-management core).** This is the single most validated loop (Joon; and it's exactly what NYU's RCT NCT06447909 isolates as the active ingredient by *removing* the reward system in the placebo arm). ([NCT06447909](https://clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT06447909))
2. **Immediate, frequent micro-rewards (coins/XP) on a variable schedule.** ADHD reward systems respond to **immediacy + unpredictability**; variable-ratio reinforcement is the most powerful conditioning schedule, and *unexpected* rewards drive curiosity and attention. ([winsomemarketing summary](https://winsomemarketing.com/marketing-and-autism/dopamine-driven-marketing-understanding-adhd-reward-systems); token-economy RCT evidence: [PMC12561863](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12561863/))
3. **A nurtured avatar/pet whose state depends on the child (care + mild loss aversion).** Joon's Doter, Habitica's avatar, EpicWin's hero — the pet's neglect-state creates gentle return pressure without nagging.
4. **Token economy redeemable for both virtual and real-world rewards.** Bridges in-app fun to real-life reinforcement; backed by token-economy literature for ADHD classroom/behavior gains. ([Frontiers token-economy pilot](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2023.1077406/full); [gamified-education RCT, 2025](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/education/articles/10.3389/feduc.2025.1668260/full))
5. **Visual, time-boxed routine scaffolding** (Brili/Goally) — externalizes time-blindness; "current step in context of whole routine" + countdown.
6. **Mastery/biofeedback loop** (Mightier) — the game itself trains self-regulation under stress; uniquely produces a *transferable skill*, not just compliance.
7. **The unsolved problem to design around: novelty decay at 4–8 weeks.** Every app's top complaint. Counter-levers seen working elsewhere: fresh/rotating content, seasonal events, collection/progression depth, **social/party play** (Habitica), streaks, and surprise drops. Build retention mechanics in from day one, not as an afterthought.

## 13. PROVEN monetization models for KIDS' ADHD apps

| Model | Examples | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| **Direct-to-consumer subscription (freemium)** | Joon ($13/mo, ~$80/yr), Brili (~$20/yr), Otsimo (~$165/yr or $230 lifetime), Habitica | **The proven winner.** Predictable revenue, parents pay; 7-day trial is standard. Sweet spot ~$80–$130/yr. |
| **Subscription + included hardware** | Mightier (~$28–40/mo incl. heart sensor), Goally (~$369–449 tablet + sub) | Works for a **premium, clinically-positioned niche**; higher trust and stickiness but lower scale and higher CAC/COGS. |
| **HSA/FSA eligibility as a payment unlock** | Mightier, EndeavorRx | Meaningful lever — lets families pay pre-tax; worth pursuing once you have a health-positioning narrative. |
| **Prescription / payer-reimbursed (PDT)** | EndeavorRx/Akili, Pear | **Avoid as primary model.** Clinically validated yet commercially fatal — payers wouldn't reimburse; Akili sold for ~$34M, Pear went bankrupt. |
| **Free, ad-free, unmonetized** | OurHome, (old) ChoreMonster | **Unsustainable** — ChoreMonster died; OurHome still has no revenue model. |
| **Paid one-time app** | EpicWin | Outdated; doesn't fund ongoing content needed to fight novelty decay. |

## 14. Direct implications for Tiny Bubbles

1. **Clone the validated core, then fix the known failures.** Joon proves the two-app contingency-management + virtual-pet loop sells. The differentiation opportunities are precisely Joon's complaint list: **(a) sustained engagement past week 8** (deeper progression, events, social/sibling/family co-op), **(b) reduced parent-verification burden** (lighter approval UX, smart defaults), **(c) screen-time guardrails**, and **(d) graceful aging-up** so families don't churn at age ~12.
2. **Monetize via consumer subscription with a free trial; price ~$80–$130/yr.** Add **HSA/FSA eligibility** early. Do **not** anchor the business on insurance/prescription reimbursement.
3. **Treat clinical evidence as a marketing/trust asset, not a payer key.** A modest published study (even a single RCT like Joon's NYU trial) materially boosts credibility with parents and clinicians — but the revenue must come from consumers.
4. **Decide deliberately on hardware.** A closed device (Goally) or sensor (Mightier) raises trust and price but caps scale; a pure phone/tablet app scales but must out-design competitors on the retention loop.
5. **Retention is the moat.** Acquisition mechanics are well understood; the company that solves **week-8 novelty decay** for ADHD kids wins the category.
